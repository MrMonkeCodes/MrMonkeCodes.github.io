Advanced Physics Laws and Principles

Classical Mechanics

  • Newton’s Laws of Motion: These three laws govern the motion of objects, defining inertia, the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration (\(F = ma\)), and the principle of action and reaction forces.
  • Law of Universal Gravitation: Every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of their distance, \(F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\).
  • Conservation Laws: Conservation of energy, momentum, angular momentum, and electric charge are foundational principles reflecting symmetries and invariants in physical systems.
  • Archimedes’ Principle: The buoyant force on an object submerged in a fluid equals the weight of fluid displaced, explaining flotation.
  • Bernoulli’s Equation: Relates pressure, velocity, and height in fluid flow, critical for aerodynamics and hydrodynamics.
  • Hooke’s Law: Describes the restoring force of springs proportional to displacement, \(F = -kx\), fundamental to harmonic oscillators.
  • Lagrangian and Hamiltonian Mechanics: Reformulations of classical mechanics focusing on energy functions and generalized coordinates, foundational for advanced mechanics and quantum theory.
  • Law of Superposition: In linear systems, the net response caused by multiple stimuli is the sum of individual responses; essential in wave mechanics and quantum states.

Electromagnetism

  • Ohm’s Law: Defines the linear relationship between voltage, current, and resistance: \(V = IR\).
  • Faraday’s Law of Induction: A changing magnetic flux induces an electromotive force proportional to the rate of change, \(\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt}\).
  • Maxwell’s Equations: Four fundamental equations unifying electricity and magnetism, describing electric fields, magnetic fields, charge, and current interactions.
  • Law of Conservation of Charge: Electric charge cannot be created or destroyed; it is conserved.
  • Kirchhoff’s Laws: Circuit laws ensuring conservation of charge at junctions and energy around loops, essential for analyzing electrical networks.
  • Snell’s Law (Refraction): Governs bending of light waves between media of different refractive indices, \(n_1 \sin \theta_1 = n_2 \sin \theta_2\).
  • Fermat’s Principle: Light travels the path of least time, explaining refraction and optical path differences.

Thermodynamics

  • First Law of Thermodynamics: Energy conservation including heat and work in a system, \(\Delta U = Q - W\).
  • Second Law of Thermodynamics: Entropy of an isolated system never decreases, implying irreversibility and directionality of time.
  • Stefan-Boltzmann Law & Blackbody Radiation: Total radiation power from a blackbody scales as the fourth power of its temperature.
  • Thermodynamic Potentials: Functions like Helmholtz and Gibbs free energy govern spontaneity and equilibrium of thermodynamic systems.

Quantum Mechanics

  • Planck’s Quantum Hypothesis: Energy is quantized in packets \(E=hf\), foundational for quantum theory.
  • Pauli Exclusion Principle: Identical fermions cannot occupy the same quantum state simultaneously, essential for atomic structure.
  • Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle: Limits precision of simultaneous measurement of position and momentum: \(\Delta x \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}\).
  • Schrödinger Equation: Governs the time evolution of quantum states via wavefunctions.
  • de Broglie Hypothesis: Particles exhibit wave-like behavior with wavelength \(\lambda = \frac{h}{p}\).
  • Quantum Tunneling: Quantum particles penetrate barriers higher than their kinetic energy due to wavefunction spread.
  • Quantum Entanglement: Correlated quantum states where measurement of one instantly affects the other, regardless of distance.
  • Casimir Effect: Attractive force between uncharged plates due to vacuum fluctuations, a manifestation of zero-point energy.
  • Quantum Field Theory (QFT): Framework combining quantum mechanics and special relativity, describing particles as field excitations.

Relativity & Cosmology

  • Einstein’s Special Relativity: Relates space and time for inertial observers; introduces time dilation, length contraction, and mass-energy equivalence (\(E=mc^2\)).
  • General Relativity: Gravity as spacetime curvature caused by mass-energy, predicting black holes and gravitational waves.
  • Einstein Field Equations: Mathematical formulation connecting matter-energy content to spacetime curvature.
  • Hubble’s Law: Expanding universe observation where galaxies’ recessional velocity is proportional to their distance.
  • Relativistic Momentum and Energy: Extensions of classical formulas accounting for near-light-speed velocities, preserving conservation laws.

Fundamental Physics & Symmetry

  • Noether’s Theorem: Every differentiable symmetry corresponds to a conservation law, linking symmetry principles to physical invariants.
  • Gauge Symmetry: Underpins interactions in particle physics, dictating the behavior of fundamental forces via symmetry groups.
